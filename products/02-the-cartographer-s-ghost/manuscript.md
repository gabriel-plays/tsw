# Quickstart

If you are reading this an hour before your session, here is the whole thing.

**The hook.** The party is traveling through familiar country when they notice something wrong with their map. A river that should flow north now flows east. A mountain range has shifted. A village they passed three days ago is now in a different location. The map in their hands is accurate — it matches what they see — but the accuracy is the problem. The land has changed to match the map, not the other way around.

**The truth.** Fifty years ago, the cartographer Aldwyn Mere died in his tower study, mid-stroke, redrawing a map of the region. His ghost never left. Every night, he works. Every time he redraws a line, the land shifts. Rivers change course. Hillsides slump. A forest becomes a meadow. The maps he makes are always perfectly accurate — they simply describe a land that is no longer the one people remember.

**The pitch to the players.** An landscape in flux. A tower where the walls are covered in maps, each one a moment in time. A ghost who cannot stop drawing. Guides who point in two different directions depending on which map you read. A place where the old world and the new world overlap, and you can see both at once.

**What can the party do?** They can find the tower and confront Aldwyn — force him to stop drawing, help him finish the map he's been working toward for fifty years, or destroy his tools entirely. They can follow the shifts and use the instability to reach a place that should be unreachable. They can gather the people who remember the old map and help them hold onto a version of the land that is slipping away. They can let the redrawing continue and simply adapt.

# The Background, in Brief

Aldwyn Mere was the finest cartographer in the region — obsessive, precise, never satisfied. He mapped every stream, every stone wall, every footpath. For forty years he refined his maps, and they became the standard by which travelers navigated. But the land was always changing in small ways — a landslide here, a new road there — and Aldwyn could never keep pace. It frustrated him. He wanted a map that would never be out of date.

On the autumn equinox, fifty years ago, Aldwyn began a final, master map. Not a record of the land as it was, but a prescription of the land as it *should be* — regularized, logical, optimized. He worked through the night, then through the next day, then for three days straight. On the fourth morning, he collapsed over the map, his pen still in hand, mid-stroke on a line that would reroute the entire eastern watershed.

He died there. But the line was never finished. And his ghost remained at the desk, unable to rest while the work was incomplete.

Every night, Aldwyn draws. Every night, the land remembers its mapmaker and shifts to match his pen. A river that ran crooked for a thousand years straightens overnight. A mountain's shadow falls in a new direction. The forest becomes geometry. The party arrives when the redrawing is accelerating — Aldwyn is growing more frantic, more certain that completion is near, and the land is convulsing in response.

# Hooks

1. **The map that changed.** The party has been using a map of the region for days. Today, looking at it again, they realize it describes a land they do not recognize. But when they look around them, the land *does* match the map. Something has shifted. The map was old but accurate. Now the map is new and still accurate. How?

2. **The conflicting guides.** Two locals who claim to know the region well give wildly different directions to the same destination. Each is certain. Each has a map — one old, one newer — and each swears by theirs. Neither is lying. The old guide's map shows a meadow; the new guide's map shows forest. When the party reaches the location, they find the forest is new, and the meadow-dweller is grieving.

3. **The tower.** A structure the party knows should not be where they see it. A tower, visible from the road, that according to every map they've seen should be fifty miles to the north. Locals either do not see it or refuse to acknowledge it. Those who do see it speak of a man inside who never stops writing.

# Approaching the Shifting Lands

The region around the tower is in a state of permanent flux. The party will notice:

- Paths that end abruptly, replaced by forest or open ground. Landmarks described in old guides that are simply gone. New landmarks that should not exist but are marked on newer maps.
- The sky seems wrong — clouds cast shadows in directions that don't match the sun. Or perhaps the sun is in two places at once.
- Sound carries strangely. Voices from villages that are no longer where they should be. The sound of a river that the party can see but not hear, or hear but not see.
- Animals behaving oddly — birds circling territories that have shifted, deer standing at boundaries between old-land and new-land as if uncertain which world to inhabit.
- Time feels off. The party might travel for what feels like an hour and arrive at a destination a day's journey away, or vice versa.

The closer to the tower, the more unstable the landscape becomes. Within a mile, the party can see both versions of the land superimposed — a ghost forest behind the real one, a phantom stream running parallel to the actual water.

# Keyed Locations

**1 — The Tower Study.** The cartographer's obsession, made physical. Three walls are completely covered in maps — layered, annotated, some pinned over others. One wall is open to the landscape, a vast window. Aldwyn sits at an oak desk, pen in hand, working by candlelight that never dims. He is translucent, more shadow than substance. He does not acknowledge the party unless they interfere with the map. His pen never stops moving.

The current map — the one he has been working on for fifty years — is almost complete. One major line remains undrawn: a ridge that would reshape the entire western quadrant. He is terrified to draw it. He is desperate to draw it. His hand trembles.

**2 — The Overlap.** A place where the old map and new map literally overlap. A field that is simultaneously meadow and forest. A stream that runs in two directions. A village that exists in two states — one abandoned, one bustling — occupying the same space but perceptible only by which map you follow. The party can move between them by looking at different maps, or by focusing their attention in specific ways.

**3 — The Compass Stone.** An ancient stone marker that once showed true north. Now it points in three directions at once. Each direction is correct, depending on which version of the land you're navigating. Locals use it to determine which map they're living in that day.

**4 — The Old Forest.** The forest as it existed forty years ago, marked on Aldwyn's earliest maps but erased by newer redrawings. The forest still exists as a ghostly duplicate — you can walk through it, but it has no substance. The trees cast no shadows. Sounds echo strangely. At night, you can see both the new forest (real, solid) and the old forest (transparent, timeless) superimposed.

**5 — The River Branching.** A place where a river that should flow in one direction splits into three. Each fork is accurately marked on a different version of Aldwyn's maps. The party must choose which river to follow, and the choice determines which version of the land they enter.

**6 — The Village of the Unmapped.** A settlement that exists in neither the old map nor the new map — it appeared in the spaces between redrawings, in the gaps Aldwyn left. The inhabitants are refugees from both old and new, people who fell through the cracks. They have built a life in the unmapped places. They fear the completion of the final map because it will erase their refuge.

**7 — The Map Room's Cellar.** Beneath the tower, filled with the detritus of fifty years of failed drafts. Crumpled parchments, ink-stained rags, abandoned tools. And in the corner, a well that appears on no map. It descends into darkness. If the party descends, they find themselves in the spaces beneath the land — the *idea* of the land before it was drawn. Aldwyn's earliest sketches are here, made manifest.

**8 — The Western Ridge.** The location where the final, undrawn line would fall. Currently, this is a place of maximum instability — the land is uncertain what it should be. Cliffs phase in and out of existence. Ravines open and close. It is the only place where the party might be able to directly influence what Aldwyn draws, or prevent him from drawing it.

# NPCs

**Aldwyn Mere, the Cartographer's Ghost.** 70 in appearance, translucent, obsessive. He does not speak unless pressed, and even then, only to clarify details about his maps. His hands never stop moving. He is not evil — he is trapped in the logic of his own work. He wants to finish. He is terrified of finishing. He will defend his maps and his work with all the strength a ghost can muster, which is considerable in a place built on his own obsession.

**Marta.** An old woman who remembers the land before Aldwyn's death. She keeps a copy of his oldest, most accurate map. She is grieving — every day, more of her memories become wrong. The landmarks she navigated by as a child are gone. Her home, marked on the old map, no longer exists; a forest has grown where it was. She is the party's potential guide to the old world, but she is also falling apart. She may ask the party to *restore* the old map, or she may ask them to *destroy* it so she can stop grieving.

**Torvin, the Map-Lost.** A young man who fell into the spaces between maps three years ago and was caught between versions. He ages in one map, de-ages in another. He exists simultaneously in two bodies, two locations. He is going mad. He knows more about the mechanics of the shifting land than anyone alive, but his knowledge is fragmentary and contradictory. He may help the party or hinder them, depending on which version of himself they're talking to.

# Resolutions

**1 — Complete the map.** The party helps Aldwyn finish the final line. His ghost finds peace. The land settles. The redrawing stops. But the final map — the one Aldwyn envisioned — becomes the permanent state of the land. The old world is erased. Marta grieves. The Village of the Unmapped is extinguished. This is the "correct" ending mechanically but devastating emotionally.

**2 — Destroy the maps.** The party burns Aldwyn's maps and tools. His ghost scatters. The land convulses one final time, then settles into a chaotic middle state — neither old nor new, but a hybrid of both. Aldwyn is finally freed, but at tremendous cost. The landscape is permanently strange.

**3 — Free Aldwyn without completing the work.** The party forces Aldwyn to *stop* drawing, by any means — destruction of pen/ink, exorcism, trapping him, negotiation. His ghost can rest unfulfilled. The land stabilizes in an incomplete state. The western ridge remains unmapped, eternally uncertain. This is the "bittersweet" ending — Aldwyn is at peace, but his work is unfinished.

**4 — Merge the maps.** The party finds a way to combine the old and new maps into a single coherent document that honors both versions. They present it to Aldwyn. He recognizes it as completion — not the completion he expected, but completion nonetheless. He rests. The land settles into a state where old and new coexist without conflict. The Unmapped Village is preserved. Marta finds peace. This ending requires the party to be creative and requires Aldwyn to be willing to accept deviation.

**5 — Take Aldwyn's place.** The party offers to take the pen. One of them must sit at the desk and continue the work. They can draw however they like — they can leave the western ridge unmapped forever, or they can redraw in service of the old map, or they can create something new. The land will obey. But one of them must draw, forever, or until another takes their place. Someone must become the new cartographer.

# The Map as Mechanic

Maps found in this adventure work as follows:

- An **old map** describes the land accurately as it was fifty years ago. Following an old map will lead you to where things *were*, which may be deeply wrong.
- A **new map** (drawn by Aldwyn recently) describes the land accurately as it currently is. But the "current" state shifts nightly.
- An **incomplete map** (like Aldwyn's work-in-progress) shows both old and new — areas he has redrawn and areas he has not yet touched. These are the safest maps to navigate by, as they describe the real state of flux.
- A **map drawn by the party** during this adventure will become temporarily accurate. The land will shift to match it. This is the only way to control the redrawing — not by stopping Aldwyn, but by offering him a different map to follow, or by drawing yourself.

**Navigation.** If the party is using a map, they must specify *which* map. Using the wrong map for the current landscape is not just navigationally wrong — it's disorienting. The party becomes unsure which version of reality they're in.

# Rumors (d6)

Roll if the party asks locals about the tower or the strange maps.

1. *"The maps change. Everyone knows it. That's why nobody uses them anymore."* (True, but locals have adapted.)
2. *"There's a man in the tower who never sleeps. He's been drawing the same map for fifty years."* (True.)
3. *"The land remembers what it was. You can see the old roads if you know where to look."* (True — the old landscape is visible as a ghost.)
4. *"My grandmother's home was erased three years ago. The cartographer redrew the map and the house just... stopped being there."* (True. Bitter.)
5. *"If you follow an old map, you'll get lost. If you follow a new map, you'll get lost differently."* (Harshly accurate.)
6. *"The tower was supposed to be demolished after Aldwyn died. But no one could find it to burn it."* (True — it shifts, exists between versions.)

# A Note for the GM

This adventure is about obsession and incompleteness. Aldwyn is not a villain — he is a man who wanted control and found that control is impossible. The maps he draws are beautiful and accurate. But accuracy and rightness are not the same thing. The party is not here to punish him; they are here to decide what *completion* means, and for whom.

The most interesting resolutions are not the ones that maximize mechanical consistency (Complete the map, Destroy the maps) but the ones that honor both the obsessive logic and the human cost (Merge the maps, Offer to take the pen). If your party finds a fourth way — a resolution you did not expect — reward them. They have understood the adventure.

The landscape is a character. Let it be strange. Let old and new coexist. Let the party feel the disorientation of not knowing which map they're following.
