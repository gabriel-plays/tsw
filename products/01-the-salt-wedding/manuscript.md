# Quickstart

If you are reading this an hour before your session, here is the whole thing.

**The hook.** The PCs pass near the village of Mire End on the night of the centennial. They hear the bells of the Pale Chapel ringing themselves. Locals will not approach the manor. A tarnished silver invitation, a hundred years out of date, finds its way into a PC's pocket.

**The truth.** A century ago, Lady Vesna of Mire End married Baron Aldric Maere to settle a salt-tax debt. At the wedding feast, her servant and lover Iosif poisoned the salt --- not to kill, but to *hold*. He was a hedge-witch with a single inherited spell. He meant to stop the wedding for a moment so Vesna could choose. The spell took. The wedding has been frozen for one hundred years. Tonight the binding fails. Whatever Vesna was about to say --- yes or no --- will be said.

**The pitch to the players.** A frozen wedding. Salt creeping like frost. A bride mid-toast, a groom mid-smile, servants halted on the stair. A locked larder. Bells ringing without ringers. Footprints in the salt that are not yours.

**What can the party do?** They can let the binding finish breaking on its own and live with whichever vow Vesna gives. They can find Iosif (still alive in a fashion, in the cellar) and either help him hold the spell or end it. They can break the binding violently --- the petrified guests will die, but the manor will be free. Or they can do the thing nobody has ever thought to do: ask Vesna what she actually wants.

# The Background, in Brief

*(For your eyes, GM. Players should learn it in pieces.)*

Lady Vesna of Mire End was sold in marriage to Baron Aldric Maere on the autumn equinox, one hundred years ago to the night, to settle a salt-tax debt her father could not pay. She was nineteen. Aldric was forty-three and not cruel by the standards of his class, which is to say he had been cruel to a tolerable number of people.

Vesna's servant Iosif had been her lover for three years. He had no claim on her, no name, no estate. He had one inherited gift from his grandmother's grandmother --- a small binding spell, salt-based, meant for keeping milk fresh and oaths kept. He brought what he had to the wedding feast. He thought, in the panic of the hour, that if he could *pause* the moment of the vow, Vesna might find a way out. He poured the binding into the salt-cellar before the toast.

The spell took more than he meant. He has been alive in the cellar for a century, salt-pickled, weeping, watching the bound moment through the cracks in the stone. He has done penance no priest set. He cannot break the spell himself --- only Vesna can.

Tonight the binding lapses. By midnight, Vesna will speak. The vow she gives will be heard by Father Olen still mid-blessing, and the wedding will complete or shatter accordingly. The manor knows. The salt knows. The bells know.

# Hooks

Pick one that fits your table.

1. **The Invitation.** A tarnished silver wedding invitation, addressed to no one, slips out of a market trader's hand and into a PC's pocket. The date written on it is one hundred years past, this very night. The trader did not know she had it.
2. **The Hire.** A descendant of the Maere family --- soft, well-fed, embarrassed --- offers the party fifty crowns to "look in on" a property whose deed has been signed over to her by a notary she cannot name. She does not say *manor*. She does not say *salt*.
3. **The Bell.** The party shelters from rain in a roadside chapel. The priest, drunk and wretched, says he can hear the bells of Mire End ringing tonight, and that the last time they rang, his grandmother stopped speaking for a month.

# Approaching Mire End

The road to Mire End runs through wet country --- peat bogs, sedge, alder. Salt-crust whitens the verges in patches. Birds will not cross over the manor's land; the party will see this if they are watching.

The village of Mire End sits a mile from the manor. Twenty-three households. A chapel without a priest (the priest fled forty years ago). An inn called The Pale Crown whose innkeep, **Dosia**, will pour the party a free drink if they say where they are going, and then refuse to speak of it further.

If the party asks Dosia about the manor:

> *"It is not haunted. Haunting is the dead bothering the living. This is the other way."*
>
> *"On the centennial nights, the bells ring themselves. We do not go up there."*
>
> *"The bride is named Vesna. We do not say her surname here."*

If the party offers her coin: she refuses it but pours them another drink.

# The Manor

Mire End Manor is two stories of grey stone with a slate roof that has not lost a tile in a hundred years. Salt has crept up the outside walls in pale tongues, like frost climbing a window. The front doors are oak, unlocked, and warm to the touch.

Inside, the silence is wrong --- not absent of sound but *full* of held sound. A breath about to be exhaled. The chandelier candles are lit and have not burned down. The salt is everywhere: drifted in corners, frosting the banisters, crunching softly underfoot.

**Footprints.** The party will, sooner or later, notice fresh footprints in the salt that are not theirs. Smaller than an adult's. Bare. They go from the cellar door into the feast hall, and back.

## Keyed Locations

**1 — Entry Hall.** A grand staircase. A serving-girl, fifteen, frozen on the third step, mid-stumble, holding a tray of cups. Her name was Mira. The cups are still warm.

**2 — The Feast Hall.** The whole of the wedding, preserved. Long oak table, thirty-two guests, the bride and groom at the head. Candles upright in their sconces. A roast pig on a platter. Lady Vesna stands in her wedding dress, mouth open mid-vow, eyes --- and this is the detail that should turn a player's stomach --- *moving*. Following the party. Aware. Aldric beside her, smiling fixed and patient. Around them, the guests in mid-laugh, mid-drink, mid-glance. Father Olen holds the blessing-book open, the page turned to the vows. The salt is thickest here. It has formed shallow drifts in the laps of the seated.

**3 — The Kitchen.** Empty of life. A pot of soup still simmering --- *still simmering*, after a hundred years. The cook's apron is folded on a chair, as if she stepped out for a moment. She did not.

**4 — The Larder.** Locked from the outside. Inside: dried herbs intact, hams uncured, and on the back wall, in salt-script that crumbles when touched, the words **HE IS BELOW**. Written by whom is unclear.

**5 — Vesna's Chamber.** Wedding-night room, bed turned down by hands that never came back. On the dresser: a folded letter in Vesna's hand, never sent, addressed to *I*. The letter says: *If you can stop this, stop it. If you cannot, forgive me.* A keen-eyed PC may notice the inkwell is still wet.

**6 — Aldric's Study.** Ledger of debts. The salt-tax debt that drove the marriage is recorded in a clerk's hand, not Aldric's. Aldric had not, in fact, demanded the marriage. He had been told, by his own steward, that *the girl was eager*. The study contains no portrait of Aldric. He did not like to look at himself.

**7 — The Chapel Annex.** Small private chapel attached to the manor. Bells ringing of their own accord. No bell-ropes are pulled. The bells are heard but cannot be seen to move. Their sound is wet --- bells ringing underwater.

**8 — The Cellar.** Stone steps down. Wine racks knocked over. Salt has filled the cellar to knee-height like fine, dry snow. At the back, half-buried in the salt: **Iosif**. Alive. A hundred years old in the body of the twenty-two-year-old he was. Salt in his hair, in his mouth, in the lines of his eyes. He has been weeping for a century. He cannot stand.

# Iosif

When the cellar is reached, Iosif speaks before the party does. His voice is thin and clear, like water over stone.

> *"You came. Which one of you is the priest? I need a priest. I need to confess and I do not have one. I have done a thing for love and I have killed thirty-two people slowly for a hundred years and I do not know if I am damned or holy and I need someone to tell me."*

He is none of those things; he is a hedge-witch boy who botched a binding. He will tell the party everything if they ask. He will beg them not to break the spell:

> *"If you break it now, she will say yes --- because she was about to say yes, because she had no other choice --- and Aldric will have her, and she will live the life I tried to save her from, and she has been awake inside the salt for a hundred years and she will know I let her."*

He will also beg the party *to* break it:

> *"If I keep holding it, she will never speak, and she will never not be nineteen, and she will stand at that table until the manor falls. What I did was take her tongue for a hundred years to keep her from saying a word she did not want to say, and I am not sure that is better."*

He will defer to whatever the party decides. He has been alone for a century. He is not capable of deciding anything.

# The Clock

From the moment the party enters the manor, you have until midnight. About three hours of real time. The bells toll the hours. Each toll, the salt cracks a little more. At midnight, Vesna speaks --- yes or no --- without intervention.

You may, as GM, advance the clock with these triggers:

- A PC opens the larder door (a quarter hour passes)
- A PC reads Vesna's letter aloud (a quarter hour)
- A PC touches Vesna (half an hour)
- A PC enters the cellar (half an hour)

This is for pace. You are not punishing the party; you are telling them the night is moving.

# Resolutions

There is no correct ending. There are five honest ones.

**1 — Let it complete.** The party does nothing. At midnight, Vesna speaks. She says, in a voice unused for a century, *"Yes."* The wedding completes. The salt sloughs off everyone. The guests resume mid-laugh and have no memory. Aldric kisses her. She lives the life. The party is ushered out as confused wedding crashers. *(This is the bleak ending. Iosif dies in the cellar within the hour, his binding spent. The manor stands for ninety more years and falls in a fire of unrelated cause.)*

**2 — Break it violently.** The party shatters the salt --- burns it, scatters it, breaks the salt-cellar at the head of the table. The binding ends. The thirty-two preserved guests die. The manor is free. The party may or may not know they have killed thirty-two people. *(Iosif lives, and goes mad within the year.)*

**3 — Free Iosif first.** The party brings Iosif up from the cellar. He stands before Vesna. He cannot speak; she cannot move. The party must intercede --- speak for him, hold his hand against her cheek, simply make the room bear witness. If they do, the binding breaks gently. Vesna falls. So does Aldric. So do the guests. The dead die quietly, in their sleep, because they have been dead a hundred years and only the salt forgot. Iosif and Vesna leave the manor together, both impossibly old, both nineteen. *(This is the ending the adventure rewards.)*

**4 — Ask Vesna.** A PC who climbs onto the table, looks her in her moving eyes, and *asks her what she wants* --- she will, with the small remaining motion of her eyes alone, indicate. (Down for no, up for yes; let the player decide what they see.) Whatever she chooses, the party may carry out. This ending is rare; players do not often think to do it. Reward them when they do.

**5 — Take her with you.** It is technically possible to physically remove the bride, in her wedding dress, like a salt-statue, from the manor. If the party does this, the binding stretches but does not break. She remains preserved. The manor decays around her absence. The party now has a frozen bride to deal with. This is the funniest ending and you should let them have it if they earn it.

# NPCs *(system-neutral)*

**Lady Vesna** --- 19, dark-haired, in a wedding dress preserved in salt. Cannot move, cannot speak. Her eyes follow. Treat as an *object* until the binding breaks; then treat as a profoundly traumatized survivor.

**Baron Aldric Maere** --- 43, stout, smiling. A man who was told what to want by his steward and trusted the telling. Petrified mid-toast. Not a villain. Not blameless.

**Iosif** --- 22 in body, 122 in soul. Servant. Hedge-witch with one inherited spell. Salt-stained, kneeling. Speaks softly. Needs a priest more than a fight.

**Father Olen** --- 60, the officiant. Holds the blessing-book. Knows nothing he should not. A neutral piece on the board.

**Mira** --- 15, the serving-girl on the stair. Will be the first guest the party finds. Her tray has not fallen for one hundred years.

**Dosia** --- innkeep at the Pale Crown in Mire End. Knows everything. Tells nothing. Pours.

**Steward Henric** --- Aldric's man. Not present at the feast (he stayed home, deliberately). Long dead by the time the party arrives, but his ledger in Aldric's study is the only honest document in the manor.

For OSR-style stat blocks: treat Iosif as a 1HD figure who cannot fight and will not flee. The petrified guests have AC as stone and 1 HP --- they break under any meaningful blow. Vesna cannot be harmed by mundane means while bound. After the binding, no one in the manor is a combatant. This is not that kind of adventure.

# Salt, as a Substance in this Adventure

Salt in Mire End is not seasoning. It is the medium of the binding, and it has small properties:

- A handful of manor-salt cast on a wound stops bleeding immediately. The wound never quite heals; a faint white seam remains.
- Manor-salt poured in a circle holds back what is held back. (The party may use this defensively at GM's discretion.)
- Manor-salt in the mouth holds a vow. A PC who eats a pinch and then swears an oath cannot break that oath without coughing salt. (This is a *gift*, and a curse, and you should let the players choose to take it.)

A small pouch of manor-salt --- six handfuls, use them once each --- is a fair reward for the gentle ending.

# Rumors Heard in Mire End *(d6)*

Roll if the party drinks at the Pale Crown.

1. *"They say the bride is awake in there. They say her eyes follow."* (True.)
2. *"My grandmother served at that wedding. She came home and never spoke again until the day she died."* (Half-true. The grandmother fled before the binding took.)
3. *"The bells ring on the centennial. Tonight is the centennial."* (True.)
4. *"A man went up there in my father's time and came down white as salt. He lived. He never married."* (True. He saw Vesna's eyes follow.)
5. *"There is a treasure in the cellar."* (Iosif.)
6. *"The wedding never happened. It was a tale the Maeres made up to cover a murder."* (False, but understandable.)

# A Note for the GM

This adventure is about being asked to choose for someone who cannot choose for themselves, and recognizing that you do not get to. The cleanest ending is the one where the party realizes Vesna is a person and not a puzzle. Reward that recognition. Do not punish the party for missing it; they are doing their best.

The villain, if there is one, is the steward Henric who told a lie a hundred years ago and is long dead and unreachable. Do not give the players a villain to fight. Give them a room to enter quietly and a girl to listen to.

If the players burn the manor down at the start, that is a valid choice. Iosif dies. Vesna dies. The thirty-two guests die. The night ends in three minutes of real time. Let it. Some tables play that way and they are not wrong.
